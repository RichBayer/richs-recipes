# Smoked Pork Butt

## Overview

A pork-forward pulled pork recipe with a sweet/savory bark and a gentle savory injection. The meat is intentionally not locked into one sauce style, so people can add sweet BBQ sauce, Carolina vinegar sauce, hot sauce, or no sauce after pulling.

Baseline recipe is for **one pork butt**, since that is the normal amount to cook. Double the injection and rub when cooking two butts.

## Equipment

- Pit Boss Brunswick vertical smoker, or similar pellet smoker
- Meat probe
- Butcher paper
- Foil pan
- Towels
- Dry cooler for resting
- Meat claws or heat-safe gloves for pulling

## Meat

- 1 pork butt, usually 7-10 lb

## Gentle savory injection

This adds moisture, salt, and light seasoning to the interior without defining the final flavor.

### Ingredients for one pork butt

- 2 cups low-sodium vegetable broth
- 1 tbsp brown sugar
- 2 tsp kosher salt
- 1 tsp garlic powder
- 1 tsp onion powder
- 1/2 tsp finely ground black pepper

If using regular salted broth, reduce the added salt to 1 tsp.

### Injection method

1. Whisk the injection until the salt and sugar dissolve.
2. Inject in a grid pattern every 1.5-2 inches.
3. Push the needle deep, press slowly, and pull the needle back as liquid goes in.
4. Expect some liquid to leak out.
5. Refrigerate the pork butt in a pan overnight if possible.

## Binder

- Plain yellow mustard

Use a thin coat. The mustard works as glue for the rub and mostly disappears during the cook. This is not a mustard-sauce recipe.

## Sweet and savory pork rub

Use the house pork rub, or make this batch for one pork butt:

- 1/4 cup brown sugar
- 2 tbsp paprika
- 2 tbsp kosher salt
- 2 tbsp coarse black pepper
- 1 tbsp garlic powder
- 1 tbsp onion powder
- 2 tsp chili powder
- 1 tsp ground mustard

No cayenne for the family version.

## Prep

1. Pat pork butt dry.
2. Trim loose fat, hard chunks, and ragged edges.
3. Inject with the gentle savory injection.
4. Refrigerate overnight if possible.
5. Before smoking, coat lightly with yellow mustard.
6. Apply rub generously on all sides.
7. Let the rubbed meat sit while the smoker preheats.

## Smoker setup

- Smoker temperature: 250°F
- Water pan: filled
- Pellets: hickory, apple, cherry, or a blend
- Probe: thickest part of the butt, away from the bone

For a deadline meal, 250°F gives a better schedule than 225°F while still producing low-and-slow pulled pork.

## Spritz

Do not spritz early. Let the bark start forming first.

Optional spritz after about 3 hours, only if the surface looks dry:

- 1/2 apple juice
- 1/2 apple cider vinegar

Spritz every 60-90 minutes only as needed. Stop spritzing once wrapped.

## Wrap

Wrap in butcher paper when both are true:

- Internal temperature is around 160-170°F
- Bark is set and does not wipe off easily

Before closing the butcher paper, a light spritz or very small splash of apple juice/apple cider vinegar is fine. Do not soak it.

## Finish

After wrapping, keep cooking until the pork reaches about 200-205°F internal.

Start checking tenderness around 198°F. The final number matters less than probe feel. The probe should slide in with very little resistance.

Typical target: 202-205°F and probe tender.

## Rest

1. Keep the pork wrapped in butcher paper.
2. Wrap the butcher-paper bundle in foil if it is leaking.
3. Wrap in towels.
4. Hold in a dry cooler.

Minimum rest: 1 hour.

Preferred rest: 2-4 hours.

If holding for a long time, keep the meat safely hot. If the internal temperature drops close to 145°F before serving, move the wrapped meat to a low oven around 170°F.

## Pulling and serving

1. Open the wrap over a pan and save the juices.
2. Pull the pork.
3. Add a small amount of reserved juices if needed.
4. Serve sauces on the side.

Good sauce options:

- Sweet BBQ sauce
- Carolina vinegar sauce
- Hot sauce
- No sauce

## Recommended cook time by meat weight

These are planning ranges for pork butt cooked around 250°F with a butcher-paper wrap during the stall. Actual time depends on the individual cut, fat content, bone, bark development, smoker behavior, weather, and how long the stall lasts.

| Pork butt weight | Estimated cook time | Recommended schedule buffer |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 5-6 lb | 6-8 hours | Start 9-10 hours before serving |
| 7-8 lb | 8-10 hours | Start 11-12 hours before serving |
| 9-10 lb | 10-12 hours | Start 13-14 hours before serving |
| 10-12 lb | 12-14+ hours | Start 15-16 hours before serving |

For a 5:00 PM meal, starting very early is better than being late. Pork butt holds well wrapped in towels inside a cooler.

## Example deadline schedule for a 5:00 PM meal

This schedule assumes a 7-10 lb pork butt and intentionally builds in rest time.

- Night before: inject and refrigerate
- 2:30 AM: start smoker at 250°F
- 2:45 AM: apply mustard binder and rub
- 3:00 AM: pork on smoker
- 6:00 AM: first bark check; spritz only if dry
- 8:00-10:30 AM: wrap when bark is set and internal temp is around 160-170°F
- 12:30-2:30 PM: likely finish window
- 2:00-4:30 PM: rest wrapped in towels inside cooler
- 4:15-4:30 PM: pull pork
- 5:00 PM: serve

If the pork is not near 190°F by noon, raise the smoker to 275°F while wrapped. If seriously behind after 1:00 PM, raising to 300°F while wrapped is acceptable.
