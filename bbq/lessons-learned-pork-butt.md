# Pork Butt Lessons Learned

Living knowledge base for smoked pork butt cooks. Cook logs record what happened. This file captures the repeatable lessons that should improve future cooks.

## Source cooks

- 2026-07-04: Two trimmed 9 lb pork butts on the Pit Boss Brunswick for America 250 BBQ.

## Core principles

### Build bark before wrapping

Do not wrap only because the pork reached a target temperature. Wrap when the bark is set, dry to the touch, and does not smear or wipe off easily.

Useful signs:

- Surface has darkened from red/brown to deeper mahogany.
- Rub feels adhered instead of wet or sandy.
- Fat has started rendering and the surface looks slightly glossy, not soaked.
- Touching lightly with a gloved finger does not smear the rub.

### Finish by tenderness, not by a single number

Internal temperature is a guide, not the final authority. Start checking around 198°F, but the pork is done when the probe slides in with very little resistance.

The July 4, 2026 cook was probe tender around 198-201°F. It did not need to be pushed to 203°F just because that was the planned target.

### Rest matters

Rest the pork wrapped in butcher paper, towels, and a dry cooler when possible. Even a shorter rest is better than no rest, but a longer rest gives the meat time to relax and hold moisture.

Minimum practical rest: 45-60 minutes.

Preferred rest: 2-4 hours.

### Save the juices

Open the butcher paper over a pan and save the juices. Add the juices back lightly when pulling, using only what the pork needs.

The juices from the paper are valuable because they carry rendered fat, seasoning, and pork flavor.

### Mix bark evenly

When pulling, mix the bark evenly through the pork so every serving gets some exterior flavor. Do not leave all bark in one pile.

### Keep sauce optional

Serve sauces on the side unless intentionally making sauced pulled pork. A good pork butt should taste good before sauce.

Useful sauce options:

- Sweet BBQ sauce
- Mild Carolina vinegar sauce
- Hot sauce
- No sauce

## Late-start recovery

A late start can be recovered if the cook is managed in phases.

1. Build bark at 250°F.
2. Wrap once bark is set.
3. Raise to 275°F after wrapping if the pork is behind schedule or stuck in the stall.
4. Raise to 300°F while wrapped only if seriously behind.
5. Finish by probe tenderness.
6. Rest as long as the schedule allows.

The July 4, 2026 cook started late because the alarm did not go off. Meat went on at 6:15 AM instead of 3:00 AM. The cook recovered by waiting for bark to set, wrapping around noon, and raising the smoker to about 275°F during the stall.

## Injection notes

The gentle vegetable-broth injection worked well.

If adding a salty seasoning blend, reduce or skip the extra kosher salt. Famous Dave's Steak & Burger seasoning was tasty but salty enough that the extra salt should probably be omitted next time.

## Trimming notes

If a pork butt has skin/rind over the fat cap, trim the rind off before injection and rub. Leave soft fat where practical so it can render during the cook.

## Equipment notes

The July 4, 2026 cook exposed a serious equipment safety concern: about an hour after pulling the meat, pellets in the Pit Boss Brunswick hopper had ignited. Treat the Brunswick as unsafe until inspected and understood.

Do not consider the Brunswick validated for future unattended cooks until the hopper-fire cause is identified and corrected.

## Repeatable checklist

Before the cook:

- Confirm alarm is actually enabled.
- Confirm pellets are dry and hopper/fire pot are clean.
- Confirm smoker is positioned away from the house and combustibles.
- Confirm probe batteries/app connectivity if using remote monitoring.
- Confirm cooler/towels are ready for resting.

During the cook:

- Do not open constantly.
- Do not spritz early.
- Wrap by bark.
- Raise temperature after wrapping if schedule requires it.
- Check tenderness in multiple spots.

After the cook:

- Rest wrapped in cooler.
- Save juices.
- Pull and mix bark evenly.
- Watch the smoker through full shutdown and cooldown.
- Confirm pellets are not smoldering or burning in the hopper.

## Open questions

- Was the Brunswick hopper fire caused by burn-back through the auger?
- Did ash buildup, airflow, or shutdown behavior contribute?
- Was the hopper fire related to running at higher temperature near the end of the cook?
- Should the Brunswick be retired rather than repaired?
